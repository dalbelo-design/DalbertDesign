Lived Experience
in Implementation

A working reference for implementation committees, frontline staff, philanthropic funders, and government agencies. It describes what meaningful inclusion of people with lived experience of homelessness requires during the implementation of a strategic action plan to reduce homelessness.

Prepared by Loren Dalbert and Loren Kadar
Lived Experience Consultants · Grounded in direct experience and informed by federal guidance from the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office of the Assistant Secretary for Planning and Evaluation (HHS/ASPE, 2021–2022).

Before You Begin
A Note Before You Read
A distinction that runs through every section of this guide
Lived Experience
Tells you what is wrong
The direct, personal knowledge of what it is like to experience homelessness: to navigate systems that were not built for you, to be turned away, to lose housing, to survive without stability. It tells you what is wrong. It surfaces the gaps that data does not capture and that people inside the system often cannot see. It cannot be replicated by study, training, or proximity.
Living Expertise
Helps you build it right
What develops when someone has taken their lived experience and refined it — through reflection, through working with systems, through understanding how decisions get made — into the capacity to actively help design better ones. It does not replace lived experience. It builds on it. The person with living expertise still knows what is wrong. But they have also developed the orientation and the skills to help build it right.

Lived experience tells you what’s wrong and points out the gaps. Living expertise helps you build it right.

Both matter. Both belong in this plan’s implementation. But they serve different functions and belong in different roles. A guide that treats them as interchangeable will produce engagement that is either performative or misdirected.

Where this guide refers to lived experience advisors in co-design and decision-making roles, it means people who have developed living expertise: people whose direct experience of homelessness has been refined into the capacity to sit at an implementation table and help build a system that works for the people they know.

Section 1
The Homelessness Context
Why standard engagement approaches often fall short here
38%
A representative example of the racial disparity in homelessness systems
In many jurisdictions, Black residents are represented in homelessness response systems at rates three to five times higher than their share of the general population — yet remain among the least represented in the rooms where policy is made. (Source: HUD 2023 Annual Homeless Assessment Report; rates vary by jurisdiction.)
People with lived experience of homelessness face specific barriers to meaningful engagement that differ from most other populations in health and human services work. Understanding those barriers is not optional background — it is the precondition for any engagement that actually works.
Grounded in practice
The approaches in this guide draw on direct consulting work with county homelessness planning processes in California, including the development of lived experience advisory structures for a countywide Strategic Action Plan to Reduce Homelessness. Informed by HHS/ASPE guidance (2021–2022) and the authors’ own intergenerational experience navigating these systems.
What makes homelessness different

The HHS/ASPE framework on engaging people with lived experience establishes sound general principles: compensate equitably, build in enough time, ensure engagement is person-centered and trauma-informed, share power genuinely. All of those apply here. But the homelessness context adds layers that generic guidance does not reach.

Institutional distrust is not irrational — it is earned

People experiencing homelessness have often had repeated encounters with systems that surveilled them, criminalized them, displaced them, or failed to deliver what was promised. Every intake form that asked the same questions as the last one. Every referral that led nowhere. Every shelter rule that made someone choose between their partner, their pet, or their belongings. Every encampment response that cleared a site without a confirmed place to go.

That history does not disappear when someone is invited to a committee meeting. Engagement that does not acknowledge it — and demonstrate something genuinely different — will not produce honest participation.

The experience of homelessness is not past tense for many people

Some of the most valuable contributors to any plan’s implementation are people who are currently experiencing homelessness, or who moved into housing recently and remain in precarious situations. The daily logistics of survival — finding food, managing health conditions without stable care, maintaining the documentation required to access services — create real barriers to participation that have nothing to do with willingness or capacity.

Engagement structures that assume a stable schedule, reliable transportation, consistent internet access, or uninterrupted availability are structures that systematically exclude the people whose perspective is most current and most needed.

Trauma is not a background condition, it is active

Homelessness is a traumatic experience that rarely ends cleanly when housing is secured. Engagement that asks people to repeatedly recount their experience, that positions them as evidence rather than experts, or that does not account for what it costs a person to show up and be a living representative of the problem causes harm. The clinical term is retraumatization. The people who experience it call it being “used.”

Meaningful engagement is strengths-based and forward-looking. It draws on what people know about how to fix what is broken — not only on what they have suffered.

Stigma shapes what people will say and to whom

People experiencing homelessness are often acutely aware of how they are perceived in any room they enter. In a room with department directors and consultants, a person with recent homelessness experience may calculate — often correctly — that candor carries risk. Saying what the system got wrong, naming the rules that excluded them, describing the navigator who could not actually help them: all of that requires a level of trust and psychological safety that does not arrive automatically. It has to be built, demonstrated, and sustained over time.

What this means for your jurisdiction

These are not reasons to delay engagement or lower ambitions for what is possible. They are the conditions that implementation structures need to be designed around.

Every plan worth implementing acknowledges that many individuals have had prior negative experiences with systems, and commits to delivering services in ways that build trust and treat people with dignity. This guide is the operational layer behind that commitment.

That starts with honesty about where your jurisdiction is. The communities most overrepresented in homelessness response systems are also communities with deep, historically rooted reasons to distrust public institutions. Acknowledgment of that history is not a detour from implementation. It is the starting point for building the kind of trust that produces genuine co-design rather than managed participation.

What trust looks like in practice
Trust in this context is built through consistency over time, not through language in a document. It is demonstrated when a lived experience advisor’s input changes a decision, and that change is named and attributed. It is demonstrated when meeting times are set around participants’ schedules rather than staff convenience. It is demonstrated when the person who raised a concern three months ago gets a follow-up that says: here is what happened with what you told us. Trust is built slowly and lost quickly.
Section 2
The Navigator-to-Cliff Problem
What lived experience tells us about service gaps

One of the most direct things lived experience can tell a system is this: there is no bridge here. There is a cliff.

Naming the problem

A navigator — whether a peer navigator, a case manager, or any other person whose job is to connect people to resources — can only be effective if the resources they are navigating toward actually exist, have capacity, and are accessible to the person in front of them. When they do not, something quietly damaging happens. The navigator is put in the position of managing a person’s disappointment at system failure. The language stays professional. The referral gets logged. The person walks away with a phone number that rings to a voicemail, a waitlist that is years long, or a shelter bed that requires conditions they cannot meet. The system records a completed contact. The person experiences a broken promise.

People who have experienced homelessness know this pattern intimately. Many stopped engaging with systems not because they stopped needing help, but because the cost of repeated disappointment became too high. That is not a motivation problem. That is a rational response to a system that was communicating, over and over, that there was nothing available while presenting that nothing as a service.

Managed disappointment
Navigators standing at the edge of a gap they cannot close, professionally obligated to keep making referrals. That is not a service delivery system. That is managed disappointment. Lived experience can see it from both sides.
Why lived experience belongs in gap identification

People with lived experience of homelessness are not just users of the navigation system. They are often its most accurate diagnosticians. They know which shelter beds are technically available but practically inaccessible because of sobriety requirements, no-couples policies, no-pets rules, or storage restrictions. They know which prevention resources run out by March. They know which referral pathways route people to organizations that no longer have capacity, or whose intake process changed in ways no one updated in the directory.

What HMIS captures
Contacts and logged referrals
Contacts and outcomes for people who engage with the system. It does not track the people who stopped engaging. It does not track the referrals that went to voicemail.
What HMIS misses
Gap intelligence
The person who was navigated to a shelter, found out there was no room for their partner, and walked back to the encampment. That intelligence lives with people who have been in that situation, or who are currently trusted by people in it.

This is where lived experience functions as gap intelligence — not as testimony about the past, but as real-time knowledge about where the system is failing right now. It is one of the most direct applications of lived experience in implementation, and one of the least used.

What this means for implementation

Lived experience advisors should be positioned as a primary source of the intelligence that feeds transparency, results tracking, and continuous resource alignment strategies: not as stakeholders to be consulted after the data has been analyzed, but as active contributors to what gets measured and what counts as a real resource versus a nominal one.

A real-time service inventory

Every jurisdiction should establish a maintained, current picture of what actually exists, what its actual capacity is, and what conditions of access apply. Not a directory of programs — a working map of where there is genuinely a bridge and where there is a cliff. Lived experience advisors, in partnership with navigators and frontline staff, are well-positioned to help build and maintain that inventory because they will catch the gaps that official program listings miss.

A structured gap report

Implementation committees should create a regular, structured input from navigators and lived experience advisors: Where are referrals failing? What resources are people being sent to that do not actually have capacity? Where are the rules of access creating de facto exclusion for specific populations? This report, reviewed on a predictable schedule, functions as an early warning system for where the plan is failing in practice — before those failures show up in outcome data, by which point people have already been harmed.

The navigator’s job should be connection, not managed disappointment
Getting there requires both a commitment to building real resources and a systematic way of knowing, in real time, where the gaps are. Lived experience embedded in implementation rather than positioned outside it is the most reliable way to generate that knowledge.
Section 3
Frontline Co-Design
Including the people who will actually do the work
Strategic plans are written by people with authority. They are implemented by people with proximity. Those are rarely the same people, and in large systems they are often separated by several layers of organizational hierarchy, multiple department lines, and very different daily realities.
The implementation gap

Implementation committees are composed of leadership from departments with key roles in homelessness response. That leadership sets strategic direction, secures resources, aligns priorities, and holds accountability across the system. That is exactly what senior leadership should do.

What senior leadership cannot do is design the actual workflows, referral pathways, data-sharing agreements, and cross-departmental protocols that implementation requires. Not because they lack commitment or intelligence, but because those workflows live at a different level of the organization.

The person who knows why a referral between two departments takes three weeks to process is not the department director. It is the intake coordinator who processes those referrals every day and has been working around the bottleneck for two years. The person who knows why a recently housed person ends up back in the system nine months later is not the division head. It is the case manager who got the call at month six and had nothing left to offer because the post-placement support had already ended.

When implementation protocols are designed without the people who will carry them out, two things happen reliably. The protocols describe the ideal state rather than the actual system. And the people who know where the design is wrong have no channel to say so until something fails visibly enough to get escalated.

What frontline co-design looks like

Frontline co-design means building a structured layer of the implementation process that includes the people who will actually do the work: program managers, case managers, intake coordinators, data staff, outreach workers, and frontline service staff across departments and partner community-based organizations — alongside people with lived experience of navigating those same systems as clients.

This is not a standing committee that produces reports. It is a working group with a specific function: to map what actually happens, surface what is broken, co-design what could work better, and test proposed changes before they are rolled out at scale.

The working group model

The basic structure is a cross-system working group: a small, mixed-level group that includes decision-making authority, operational knowledge, and lived experience in the same room, working on a specific problem with a sustained weekly rhythm. The group does not replace the implementation committee. It operates within the strategic frame set by leadership and reports findings upward. But it has real authority over how implementation protocols are built.

This structure has been used in practice to build a direct cross-system connection between a homeless services provider and a county workforce system — a connection that did not previously exist. That process brought together executive leadership, frontline staff, and a lived experience consultant from each organization, meeting weekly on a specific problem until the pathway was designed and functional. What it demonstrated is that new cross-system connections do not get built in committee meetings. They get built when the right people — with decision-making authority, operational knowledge, and lived experience — are in a room together consistently, working on something specific enough to make real.

Discovery sprints: mapping what is actually broken

The working group’s primary tool is the discovery sprint: a structured, time-limited session in which frontline staff from two or more departments, together with people with lived experience, map a specific shared process end to end. How does a person leaving interim housing get connected to prevention resources? What happens when a justice-involved person needs a housing referral? Where does the hand-off between departments actually break down, and why?

The sprint surfaces specific friction points — the forms that no longer exist, the systems that do not communicate, the rules from one department that create barriers in another, the referral pathways that technically exist but practically do not. It also surfaces the workarounds that frontline staff have already invented, which are often the seeds of better protocols.

Discovery sprints produce something that strategy documents cannot: a ground-level map of where collaboration actually needs to be built. Not where it should exist according to the plan, but where the real gaps are and what connecting them would actually require.

Why lived experience belongs in the working group — not just the committee

Federal research on engaging people with lived experience is clear that the most impactful engagement happens when people are involved throughout the entire decision-making process — including at implementation and evaluation levels — not only at the advisory level. Placing lived experience only at the senior committee level means it shapes strategy but not practice. The place where strategy becomes practice is the frontline working group.

A frontline staff member can tell you when a referral breaks down from the worker’s side. A person with lived experience can tell you what it felt like from the other side. Both perspectives are required to understand why a process fails. Neither is sufficient alone.

The combination of community intelligence with the operational knowledge of frontline staff is where genuinely new cross-departmental connections get discovered. Not in a committee meeting — in a room where an outreach worker and an eligibility specialist compare notes for the first time and realize they have been serving the same person through two systems that have never spoken to each other.

Section 4
Deep Inclusion
What meaningful lived experience participation in this plan actually requires
The difference between a seat and a voice

Most homelessness plans commit to maintaining seats for people with lived experience of homelessness on implementation committees. That commitment is meaningful. It is also incomplete on its own, because a seat is a structural arrangement and a voice is a relational and institutional one. The two are not the same, and conflating them is how lived experience engagement most commonly fails.

01
Preparation support
A seat without preparation support is a seat where the person is expected to walk into a room with department directors, engage with policy and budget language they have not encountered before, and contribute meaningfully under time pressure — without the context, documents, or relationship-building that staff in that same room have had for months.
02
Compensation
A seat without compensation is a seat that systematically excludes people who are economically precarious. Federal guidance from HHS/ASPE is explicit: compensation must be commensurate with rates paid to other experts. Anything less signals that lived experience is a volunteer contribution rather than a professional expertise.
03
Decision-making scope
A seat without decision-making scope is an advisory arrangement, not a co-design one. The plan should specify what domains lived experience seats have documented influence over, how input is recorded, and how the committee communicates what it did and did not act on — and why.
The archetype question: who belongs in which role

Not all people with lived experience of homelessness are suited for every role in a plan’s implementation. This is not a judgment about the value of anyone’s experience. It is a recognition that different roles require different orientations, and that placing the wrong type of contributor in the wrong role does a disservice to everyone, including the contributor.

A practical framing for selection
The question for the implementation committee is not only: has this person experienced homelessness? It is: has this person developed the orientation and the skills to translate that experience into design thinking? Can they hold the community’s perspective and engage productively with systems actors at the same time? Are they oriented toward building, not only naming?
What the development of living expertise requires in practice

When a person with lived experience is brought into an implementation role, the jurisdiction has a responsibility to support the development of their living expertise, not just to extract what they already know.

This means genuine orientation: not a packet of documents sent the day before the first meeting, but a structured introduction to the plan, its strategies, the relevant systems and funding structures, and the decision-making processes of the committee. It means preparation time before each meeting so that advisors can review materials, formulate questions, and arrive ready to contribute.

It means pairing experienced advisors with a staff point of contact who can answer questions, explain jargon, and help navigate the institutional dynamics of the committee. It means ensuring that the logistical arrangements of the engagement — meeting times, location, format, stipend payment timelines — are designed with participants’ circumstances in mind, not the institution’s convenience.

It also means protecting advisors from one of the most damaging dynamics in this kind of engagement: being the only person in the room expected to speak for an entire population. No single person with lived experience of homelessness represents all people experiencing homelessness. Jurisdictions should recruit multiple advisors with diverse experiences, different pathways into homelessness, different geographic areas, different demographic backgrounds, and different system encounters — so that no one person carries the weight of representation alone.

Avoiding tokenization and the same-voices problem

Federal research identifies two recurring failure modes in lived experience engagement. The first is tokenization: bringing someone in to fulfill the appearance of inclusion without giving them meaningful influence over decisions. The second is the same-voices problem: repeatedly engaging the same individuals, which excludes the diversity of experience that makes community input valuable and places an unsustainable burden on the people who keep being asked.

Avoiding tokenization

Tokenization is avoided not by sincerity of intention but by structure. Jurisdictions should establish in writing what decisions lived experience advisors will be consulted on before they are finalized. They should create a documented response process: when an advisor raises a concern or recommendation, the committee records it, deliberates on it, and reports back in writing what it decided and why. When input was acted on, that should be named and attributed. When it was not, the reason should be explained honestly — including when the constraint is a resource or policy limitation the committee cannot control.

Avoiding the same-voices problem

Jurisdictions should not rely on the same individuals throughout the implementation process. They should actively seek out people whose perspectives have not yet been included — people from communities and geographies underrepresented in engagement; people who have recently exited homelessness and have current knowledge of how the system functions right now; people from communities that are statistically overrepresented in the data but structurally excluded from formal engagement processes.

The pre-implementation review

One of the most concrete and underused applications of living expertise in implementation is the pre-implementation review: a structured process in which experienced advisors review proposed protocols, procedures, and service access standards before they are finalized, not after they have been rolled out and are already failing.

Before any procedure is finalized — encampment response protocols, low-barrier shelter standards, prevention targeting criteria, navigation referral pathways — it should be reviewed by people with current or recent lived experience of those specific systems. The review is structured: What works about this? What would prevent people you know from engaging with it? What would need to change for this to actually reach the people it is designed to serve?

The responses are documented, shared with the committee, and incorporated or explicitly responded to before the protocol goes live. This is not a veto. It is a quality check — the same quality check any well-designed system applies before implementation, with the specific expertise that only living expertise can provide.

A Closing Note
This guide is not a finished document.

Implementation is not a fixed process. The people closest to the work — frontline staff and people with lived experience — will learn things in the first year that no planning document could anticipate. This guide should be treated as a living reference: reviewed, revised, and updated as implementation proceeds, with input from the advisors and staff who are doing the work.

The distinction this guide returns to throughout — lived experience tells you what is wrong, living expertise helps you build it right — is not a hierarchy. It is a development process every jurisdiction has an obligation to support, not just benefit from. Investing in the conditions that allow people with lived experience to develop living expertise is investing in better outcomes for the people it is designed to serve.

The goal is not inclusion for its own sake. The goal is a homelessness response system that works for the people experiencing it, not just the people administering it. Lived experience and living expertise, embedded throughout implementation, are how you close the distance between those two things.
